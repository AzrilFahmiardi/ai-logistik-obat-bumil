# Dataset Documentation: Maternal Drug Stock Forecasting

## Overview

This document describes the construction of `maternal_dataset_filled.csv`, a panel dataset used to train an XGBoost model for one-month-ahead forecasting of drug closing stock across ten public health facilities (Puskesmas) in Indonesia. The dataset covers 30 maternal health drugs over 24 months (March 2023 to February 2025), yielding 7,200 rows in total (10 facilities x 30 drugs x 24 periods).

The dataset is synthetic-realistic: its structure and static attributes are grounded in real administrative data and peer-reviewed research, while the two target variables (`closing_stock` and `estimated_total_cases`) were generated by a large language model conditioned on quantitative summaries extracted from those real sources.

---

## Source Materials

### 1. Puskesmas Bareng Dispensing Records (Excel)

The primary empirical anchor is a set of Excel files containing monthly drug stock transaction data from Puskesmas Bareng, a public health facility in Malang, East Java, Indonesia. The files cover outpatient pharmacy dispensing (`keluar`, stock out) and warehouse replenishment (`masuk`, stock in) across five months in 2024: January, March, April, July, and November.

Key statistics extracted from these records:

- Total dispensing transactions: 23,836 across 144 unique drugs
- Total replenishment transactions: 249 across 102 unique drugs
- The three highest-volume drugs by units dispensed were Amlodipine 10 mg (35,126 units), Metformin 500 mg (34,731 units), and Amlodipine 5 mg (31,317 units), confirming the dominance of chronic disease medication in outpatient demand
- Monthly dispensing volume ranged from 62,211 units in April (lowest) to 131,361 units in November (highest), a surge of approximately 87 percent above the monthly average
- The November spike was concentrated in symptomatic drugs: Paracetamol showed a demand ratio of approximately 700x relative to other months, Chlorpheniramine (CTM) 690x, and Sodium Diclofenac 595x, consistent with peak dry-to-wet season disease burden in Indonesia
- Replenishment was delivered in large batches from internal warehouse sources (Gudang Obat Puskesmas, ~60%; Gudang Farmasi Kota, ~4%), with an average of 1,239 units per inbound transaction
- 45 of the 144 drugs dispensed had no corresponding replenishment record, indicating either unrecorded internal transfers or latent stockout risk

These observations were used to calibrate the demand shape, seasonal multipliers, and supply patterns in the generated dataset.

### 2. BMJ Global Health Paper (Fanda et al., 2026)

The second source is the peer-reviewed study "Local systems, local solutions: which factors drive essential medicine availability in public health facilities across Indonesia?" published in BMJ Global Health. The paper analyzed medicine availability data from a nationally representative sample of Indonesian Puskesmas and identified structural factors that predict whether essential drugs are in stock on any given day.

Key findings used to inform the dataset:

- Facilities in Java, including East Java, had a mean medicine availability rate of approximately 68.89 percent, higher than facilities in eastern Indonesia
- The supply chain from district health office (DHO/Dinas Kesehatan) to primary health center (PHC/Puskesmas) is the dominant distribution pathway; most Puskesmas depend entirely on the DHO warehouse for resupply
- Lead time, geographic remoteness, and rainy-season road access are independently associated with stockout risk
- The LPLPO (Laporan Pemakaian dan Lembar Permintaan Obat) reporting system is the standard instrument for monthly drug requests, but recording gaps are common in remote facilities
- The paper argues for 18-month demand planning cycles (DRP) to buffer against seasonal demand spikes, which is directly relevant to the November surge pattern observed in the Bareng records

These findings informed the assignment of facility-level attributes (lead time, accessibility score, rainy season access category) and the relative demand scaling applied to remote versus urban facilities in the generated data.

### Step 2: Build the Raw Panel Skeleton

A full panel of all facility-drug-period combinations was generated programmatically: 10 facilities × 30 drugs × 24 monthly periods (March 2023 to February 2025), yielding 7,200 rows. Each row carries the static facility and drug attributes as fixed columns. The two time-varying columns, `closing_stock` and `estimated_total_cases`, were initialized as null and filled in the next step.

The raw skeleton was saved as `maternal_dataset_raw.csv`.

### Step 3: Summarize Real Data into a Context JSON

The dispensing and replenishment records from Puskesmas Bareng were analyzed and compressed into a structured JSON file (`supply_chain_summary_puskesmas_bareng.json`). This file captures:

- Monthly total demand by month name, including the November spike
- Top five drugs by total units dispensed
- High-risk drugs with no replenishment records (proxy stockout candidates)
- Supply source distribution (Gudang Obat Puskesmas, Gudang Farmasi Kota, etc.)
- Average units per inbound and outbound transaction
- The November seasonality ratios for each symptomatic drug category
- Alignment commentary linking the Bareng data to the Fanda et al. findings (e.g., LPLPO gaps, supply chain structure, seasonal DRP recommendations)

This JSON served as the generation prompt context for the LLM step.

### Step 4: LLM-Assisted Generation of Target Variables

Claude Sonnet 4.6 was used to populate `closing_stock` and `estimated_total_cases` for all 7,200 rows. The model received the context JSON as a conditioning document and applied the following generation logic:

**Estimated total cases** was computed per row as a function of:

- A drug-specific base monthly demand, calibrated against the Bareng dispensing totals (e.g., OBT-001 base ~1,800 units/month; OBT-030 base ~125 units/month)
- A monthly seasonal multiplier, derived from the Bareng monthly totals: April = 0.80 (lowest), November = 1.87 (baseline surge), with symptomatic drugs receiving an additional within-November multiplier (Paracetamol x5.5, CTM-equivalent x5.3, NSAID-equivalent x4.8, etc.)
- A facility size multiplier derived from the accessibility score: score 1 = 0.55x, score 2 = 0.70x, score 3 = 0.85x, reflecting the smaller catchment population of remote facilities
- Uniform random noise in the range [0.88, 1.12] to introduce row-level variance

The minimum enforced value was 5 units per row.

**Closing stock** was computed per row as a fraction of that month's estimated demand, simulating a target months-of-stock (MOS) position:

- Accessibility score 4-5 (urban, not present in this dataset but used as reference): 1.8 to 3.0 MOS
- Accessibility score 3 (ordinary): 1.2 to 2.2 MOS
- Accessibility score 2 (remote): 0.6 to 1.5 MOS
- Accessibility score 1 (very remote): 0.3 to 1.1 MOS

MOS targets were reduced by 50 percent in November (demand surge depletes stock faster) and by 25 percent in October (pre-surge depletion). Random noise in [0.85, 1.15] was applied. The minimum enforced value was 1 unit per row.

No row in the final dataset contains a zero or null value in either column.

## Intended Use

The dataset was constructed to train and evaluate a global XGBoost model for one-month-ahead closing stock forecasting at the facility-drug level. The forecasting task is defined as: given historical stock and demand data up to month t, predict closing stock at month t+1.

Key modeling considerations that the dataset is designed to support:

- Lag features (1, 2, 3, 6, 12 months) of closing stock capture carry-over inventory dynamics
- Lag features of estimated cases and derived consumption capture demand signal
- The November seasonal spike is explicitly learnable via month encoding, binary season flags, and interaction terms between month and cases
- Accessibility-based variation across facilities creates meaningful cross-sectional signal for the model
- The log1p-transformed target reduces the influence of high-stock-month outliers during training

The dataset is not intended to be treated as ground truth for any real facility. It is a structured synthetic dataset designed to have realistic statistical properties for the purpose of supply chain forecasting model development and evaluation.

---

## Limitations

- The real data covers only one facility (Puskesmas Bareng) and five months in a single year. Demand patterns at other facilities may differ substantially.
- The generation process does not simulate stockout events: closing stock is always positive, which means the model trained on this data will not learn stockout-recovery dynamics.
- Replenishment timing is not modeled explicitly. The dataset captures closing stock as an outcome but does not include inbound stock quantities or order dates as features.
- LLM-generated values are internally consistent by construction but are not validated against actual records from the ten simulated facilities.
- The drug list reflects a maternal health formulary and does not represent the full essential medicine list (Fornas) used at Indonesian Puskesmas.
